## 1. WHAT ARE SURPLUS FUNDS

### 1.1 Foreclosure Surplus

When a foreclosed property is sold at a foreclosure sale (sheriff's sale, trustee's sale, or auction), the sale proceeds are applied in the following order:

1. **Costs of sale** (auctioneer fees, advertising, trustee fees)
2. **First mortgage/senior lien** (principal, interest, fees, costs)
3. **Junior liens** (second mortgages, HELOCs, judgment liens, tax liens)
4. **Remainder (SURPLUS)** to the former property owner (mortgagor/grantor)

The "surplus" is the amount remaining after all liens and costs are paid. This money legally belongs to the former homeowner. Surplus amounts range from a few hundred dollars to hundreds of thousands of dollars depending on property value, mortgage balance, and sale price.

**Sources of foreclosure surplus:**
- **Judicial foreclosure sales:** Court-supervised sales where the court distributes proceeds
- **Non-judicial foreclosure sales:** Trustee-conducted sales where the trustee or deed of trust holder distributes proceeds
- **Tax foreclosure sales:** Sales for unpaid property taxes where surplus belongs to former owner
- **HOA foreclosure sales:** Sales for unpaid homeowners association assessments

### 1.2 Tax Sale Surplus

When a property is sold at a tax lien sale or tax deed sale for nonpayment of property taxes:
- **Tax Lien States:** The sale is of a lien, not the property. Surplus is the excess bid amount over the lien amount. Owner retains title and must redeem.
- **Tax Deed States:** The property itself is sold. Surplus is the excess over taxes and costs. Former owner has a right to surplus.
- **Hybrid States:** Some states use both systems.

### 1.3 Other Types of Surplus Funds

- **Eminent domain surplus:** Excess condemnation awards after paying encumbrances
- **Partition sale surplus:** Proceeds from court-ordered property division sales
- **Bankruptcy surplus:** Assets remaining after creditor distributions
- **Estate surplus:** Assets remaining after probate distributions

## 2. THE SURPLUS FUNDS RECOVERY BUSINESS MODEL

### 2.1 How It Works

The surplus funds recovery business identifies individuals who are owed surplus funds from foreclosure or tax sales and helps them claim those funds. The business typically works as follows:

**Phase 1: Identification**
- Monitor foreclosure sale notices, auction results, and court dockets
- Identify sales that generated surplus proceeds
- Research ownership records to identify the claimant

**Phase 2: Claimant Location**
- Locate the former homeowner through public records, skip tracing, or database searches
- Verify identity and entitlement to funds

**Phase 3: Engagement**
- Contact the potential claimant
- Explain the existence of surplus funds and the claim process
- Enter into a written agreement:
  - **Authorization agreement:** Authorizes the company to act on claimant's behalf
  - **Assignment agreement:** Claimant assigns a portion of surplus to the company
  - **Fee agreement:** Specifies the fee structure (typically percentage-based)

**Phase 4: Claim Processing**
- Prepare and file necessary court documents
- Obtain court order directing disbursement of funds
- Collect funds from the court or trustee
- Disburse funds: pay claimant their share, retain company fee

**Phase 5: Compliance**
- Report fees paid (where required)
- Maintain records
- File unclaimed property reports if applicable

### 2.2 Fee Structures

| Fee Type | Description | Typical Range |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Finder's Fee** | Fee for identifying and connecting claimant with recovery resources | 15-35% of recovered amount |
| **Assignment Percentage** | Claimant assigns a percentage of their surplus rights in exchange for assistance | 20-40% |
| **Contingency Fee** | Fee is contingent on successful recovery (similar to attorney contingency) | 25-40% |
| **Flat Fee** | Fixed fee for services regardless of recovery amount | Less common |
| **Referral Fee** | Fee for referring claimant to an attorney or service provider | Varies by state ethics rules |

### 2.3 Operational Models

| Model | Description | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Direct Finder** | Company contracts with claimant, prepares documents, files claims directly | Tier 1 states |
| **Facilitated Attorney** | Company identifies claimants, refers to partner attorney, attorney handles claim | Tier 2 states |
| **Full Attorney Model** | Attorney directly contracts with claimant, company provides administrative support | Tier 3 states |
| **Pure Referral** | Company refers all potential claimants to attorneys, no direct engagement | States with restrictive laws |

## 3. LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Property Rights Framework

Surplus funds represent a property interest belonging to the former property owner. Key legal principles:

- **Constitutional Protection:** Surplus funds are property protected by the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments (due process, takings)
- **Statutory Entitlement:** State statutes define the right to surplus and the claim process
- **Equitable Interest:** Courts of equity oversee surplus distribution in judicial foreclosure states
- **Contractual Rights:** The right to surplus can be assigned, conveyed, or encumbered (subject to state law restrictions)

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** The property rights framework for surplus funds varies significantly by state. Consult local counsel regarding the nature of the property interest in each jurisdiction.

### 3.2 Due Process Considerations

- Claimants must receive adequate notice of surplus funds availability
- Notice requirements vary by state (publication, mail, posting)
- Failure to provide proper notice may invalidate the foreclosure or surplus distribution
- Courts may appoint a receiver or guardian ad litem for missing or incapacitated claimants
- Due process requires that surplus distributions be reasonable and not unconscionable

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** Ensure your claim process provides sufficient notice and opportunity to be heard consistent with constitutional requirements.

### 3.3 Assignment Law

The assignment of surplus rights is governed by:

- **Common Law Assignment:** Generally, any property right (including the right to surplus) may be assigned unless prohibited by statute or public policy
- **Statutory Restrictions:** Some states limit or prohibit assignment of surplus rights (see Tier 3 states)
- **Champerty and Maintenance:** The common law doctrine against buying an interest in litigation. Some states apply this to surplus assignments if litigation is pending or contemplated
- **Usury:** Fee structures that exceed legal interest rates may violate usury laws
- **Equitable Assignment:** Courts may recognize assignments in equity even if not in strict compliance with formalities

**Requirements for valid assignment (generally):**
- In writing
- Signed by assignor
- Reasonably identifies the right assigned
- Supported by consideration (not always required for validity)
- Notarized (required in some states)
- Witnessed (required in some states)

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** Assignment requirements vary significantly. Have assignment forms reviewed in each state of operation.

### 3.4 Finder's Fee Doctrine

The finder's fee doctrine governs compensation paid to a person who identifies an opportunity and connects parties. Key considerations:

- **No License Required (generally):** Finding or referring business opportunities typically does not require a real estate or securities license if the finder does not negotiate or participate in the transaction
- **However:** Some states have expanded the definition of "real estate brokerage" to include surplus recovery
- **Reasonableness:** Fees must be reasonable and not unconscionable
- **Disclosure:** Full disclosure of fees is universally required
- **Consumer Protection:** Finder fees are subject to state consumer protection laws

**When a finder's fee may be illegal:**
- If it constitutes unlicensed practice of law
- If it violates state-specific surplus recovery restrictions
- If it constitutes an illegal referral fee under state ethics rules
- If it violates state usury or lending laws
- If disclosure is inadequate

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** Ensure fee structures comply with each state's finder fee and consumer protection requirements.

### 3.5 Unauthorized Practice of Law (UPL)

The most significant legal risk for the surplus recovery business is UPL:

**What may constitute UPL in surplus recovery:**
- Preparing legal documents (petitions, motions, orders) for another person
- Giving legal advice about surplus rights, foreclosure procedures, or claim strategies
- Appearing in court on behalf of another person
- Negotiating legal rights or settlement of claims
- Selecting and completing court forms with legal consequences

**Safe harbor activities (generally not UPL):**
- Identifying publicly available information
- Referring claimants to licensed attorneys
- Providing administrative support to attorneys
- Factual research and data analysis
- Explaining publicly available court procedures
- Providing pre-printed forms for claimant to complete with attorney supervision

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** UPL statutes vary by state. The line between permissible assistance and UPL is jurisdiction-specific. Have your operational model reviewed in each state.

### 3.6 Consumer Protection Laws

Surplus recovery is subject to general consumer protection laws:

- **Federal:** FTC Act (Section 5) — unfair and deceptive acts; Telemarketing Sales Rule; TCPA; FDCPA (if applicable)
- **State:** Consumer protection acts (Little FTC Acts) — each state has its own
- **State-specific:** Some states have laws specifically targeting foreclosure-related services

**Common requirements:**
- No false or misleading representations
- Clear disclosure of all fees
- Right to cancel (cooling-off period) in some transactions
- No harassment or coercive conduct
- Proper identification of business and purpose of contact
- Written contracts with specific terms

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** Consumer protection compliance is essential. Review all marketing and solicitation materials.

## 5. COMPLIANCE CHECKLIST PER TRANSACTION TYPE

### 5.1 Standard Finder/Assignment Transaction (Tier 1 States)

**Pre-Engagement:**
- [ ] Verify surplus exists (confirm with court, trustee, or county records)
- [ ] Confirm amount of surplus
- [ ] Verify claimant identity (government-issued ID)
- [ ] Confirm claimant is the rightful owner/heir (title search, probate records if deceased)
- [ ] Check for competing claims (other heirs, judgment creditors, bankruptcy)
- [ ] Determine if claimant is competent to contract
- [ ] Check for active bankruptcy (automatic stay applies)

**Engagement:**
- [ ] Provide written disclosure of:
  - Existence and amount of surplus
  - Services to be provided
  - Fee amount and basis (percentage, flat fee, other)
  - Any third-party fees (filing fees, service fees, etc.)
  - Estimated timeline
  - Claimant's right to pursue claim independently
- [ ] Execute written authorization/assignment agreement
  - Signed by claimant
  - Dated
  - Notarized (where required)
  - Witnessed (where required)
  - Copies provided to claimant
- [ ] No contract for more than 50% of surplus (avoid unconscionability)
- [ ] Provide notice of cancellation rights (where applicable by state law)

**Claim Processing:**
- [ ] Prepare claim documents per court requirements
- [ ] Verify filing fees and procedure
- [ ] File claim with appropriate court or trustee
- [ ] Serve all interested parties (if required)
- [ ] Attend hearing (if required)
- [ ] Obtain court order directing disbursement
- [ ] Collect funds
- [ ] Disburse per agreement

**Post-Claim:**
- [ ] Provide accounting to claimant
- [ ] Retain records for minimum 7 years
- [ ] Report fees if required by state
- [ ] File unclaimed property report if funds remain unclaimed

### 5.2 Attorney-Facilitated Transaction (Tier 2 States)

- [ ] All Standard Finder items, PLUS:
- [ ] Attorney engagement letter with claimant (separate from finder agreement)
- [ ] Attorney fee agreement compliant with state ethics rules
- [ ] Attorney supervises or reviews all legal documents
- [ ] Finder agreement clearly separated from attorney engagement
- [ ] Attorney handles filing and court appearances
- [ ] Fee split between attorney and finder complies with state ethics rules (Model Rule 1.5(e))
- [ ] Client consents to fee split in writing
- [ ] Total fee (attorney + finder) is reasonable

### 5.3 Attorney-Driven Transaction (Tier 3 States)

- [ ] Attorney directly contracts with claimant
- [ ] Attorney fee is subject to state ethics rules (contingency fee caps apply)
- [ ] Finder/support company has NO direct contract with claimant
- [ ] Company provides only:
  - Identification of surplus opportunities
  - Referral of claimant to attorney
  - Administrative support to attorney
- [ ] Company charges attorney at fair market value for services
- [ ] Per-case referral fees to non-attorneys are PROHIBITED (Model Rule 5.4)
- [ ] Marketing services provided to attorney on bona fide retainer or flat fee (not per-referral)

## 6. PROHIBITED PRACTICES BY STATE

### 6.1 Activities Prohibited in Multiple States

| Practice | Prohibited In | Basis |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| **Pre-sale solicitation** | CA, FL, MA, NY | Soliciting assignment of surplus before foreclosure sale may be void/voidable |
| **Excessive fees (>50%)** | CA, CT, FL, NJ, NY | May be unconscionable or violate fee caps |
| **Non-attorney legal practice** | All states (varies scope) | UPL statutes — cannot prepare legal documents or give legal advice |
| **Assignment of future surplus** | CA, NY, FL (restricted) | Pre-sale assignment of surplus may be champertous |
| **Referral fees to non-attorneys** | CA, FL, MA, NY, NJ | Model Rule 5.4 adopted (varies) |
| **False/misleading advertising** | All states | Consumer protection laws |
| **Direct solicitation of foreclosure victims** | CA, MA, NY, FL | Consumer protection regulations |
| **No written contract** | CA, FL, NY | Statute of frauds, consumer protection |

### 6.2 State-Specific Prohibitions

| State | Specific Prohibition |
|-------|---------------------|
| **California** | Pre-sale assignment of surplus; non-attorney claim filing; automatic dialing/marketing restrictions; CCPA compliance for data use |
| **Florida** | Solicitation of foreclosure victims without license; pre-sale surplus assignments; certain fee arrangements |
| **Louisiana** | Non-attorney legal practice strictly limited (civil law system); authentic act requirements |
| **Massachusetts** | Finder fees without disclosure; MGL 93A violations (broadly defined); 940 CMR regulations |
| **New Jersey** | CFA violations; chancery court procedures strict; fee arrangements subject to court audit |
| **New York** | Non-attorney involvement in claims; referral fees to non-attorneys; pre-sale assignments void |

### 6.3 Federally Prohibited Practices

- **TCPA violations:** Autodialed calls/texts without consent (or to numbers on DNC registry)
- **Telemarketing Sales Rule:** Required disclosures, calling hour restrictions, do-not-call compliance
- **FDCPA violations:** If engaged in "debt collection" as defined by federal law (may apply if collecting surplus as assignee)
- **FTC Act Section 5:** Unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce
- **CAN-SPAM Act:** Commercial email requirements
- **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act:** Privacy and data security (if handling financial information)

### 8.3 Escheat Process

When surplus funds are not claimed within the statutory period, they are remitted to the state's unclaimed property division. The process:

1. **Holder reporting:** Court or trustee must report unclaimed surplus to state
2. **Publication:** State publishes list of unclaimed property owners
3. **Claim period:** Owners may still claim from the state (often without time limit)
4. **State use:** States use unclaimed property funds for general revenue (with claims paid from ongoing collections)

**Operational implication for escheated funds:**
- Claimants can still recover escheated funds from the state's unclaimed property division
- However, the state does not typically pay finder fees — the business model may not work for already-escheated funds
- Some states have "finders" programs that allow recovery assistance for escheated property (with registration)

## 9. RISK TIERS EXPLAINED WITH OPERATIONAL IMPLICATIONS

### 9.1 Tier 1 — Favorable (35 States)

**Composite Score:** 75-100

**Characteristics:**
- Clear statutory framework for surplus distribution
- No prohibition on assignment of surplus rights
- No statutory caps on finder fees
- Non-attorney filing permitted (pro se, or on behalf of claimant with authorization)
- Public court records accessible
- Escheat periods of 3-5+ years
- No known regulatory enforcement against surplus recovery

**Operational Implications:**
- **Standard business model is viable**
- No attorney required for claims (though attorney review of forms recommended)
- County-level procedures can be learned and systematized
- Fee structures of 25-40% are generally acceptable
- Focus on building volume and process efficiency
- Compliance investment: Moderate

**Risk Factors:**
- County-level variation in court procedures
- Future legislative changes possible
- Consumer protection laws still apply

**Go/No-Go Decision:** GO — Deploy standard operating model

### 9.2 Tier 2 — Moderate (12 States)

**Composite Score:** 45-74

**Characteristics:**
- Some restrictions on assignment or finder fees
- Attorney involvement preferred or strongly recommended
- Enhanced disclosure requirements
- Some bulk data access limitations
- Escheat periods vary (2-7 years)
- Some enforcement activity or regulatory guidance

**Operational Implications:**
- **Enhanced compliance model required**
- Local counsel should be engaged on retainer
- State-specific authorization/disclosure forms required
- Court approval of fees may be needed
- County-level restrictions may require local counsel
- Compliance investment: High (but manageable)

**Risk Factors:**
- UPL risk if too much hands-on involvement
- Fee disclosure and reasonableness scrutiny
- Local court rules may be more restrictive than state law
- Consumer enforcement risk

**Go/No-Go Decision:** CONDITIONAL GO — Requires local counsel engagement and enhanced compliance

### 9.3 Tier 3 — Restricted/Prohibited (6 States)

**Composite Score:** 0-44

**Characteristics:**
- Significant restrictions on assignment
- Finder fees capped or prohibited
- Attorney required for all claim activities
- Aggressive regulatory enforcement
- Short escheat periods (CA, FL, NJ: 2-3 years)
- State-specific laws that target the business model
- High UPL risk

**Operational Implications:**
- **Standard business model is NOT viable**
- Full attorney-driven model required (no direct company-claimant contract)
- Company role limited to identification, referral, and administrative support
- No fee splitting with non-attorneys (Model Rule 5.4)
- Marketing services model as alternative
- Compliance investment: Very high

**Risk Factors:**
- Actual enforcement risk (fines, cease-and-desist, criminal UPL charges)
- Reputational damage from enforcement actions
- High cost of attorney network development
- Limited volume potential in attorney-driven model

**Go/No-Go Decision:** NO for standard model; CONDITIONAL for attorney-driven model with enhanced compliance

## APPENDIX A: GLOSSARY OF TERMS

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Assignment** | Transfer of rights from one person to another |
| **Assignee** | Person who receives assigned rights |
| **Assignor** | Person who transfers rights |
| **Champerty** | Illegal agreement where one party shares in litigation proceeds without having a legitimate interest |
| **Claimant** | Person entitled to surplus funds |
| **Contingency Fee** | Fee contingent on successful recovery |
| **Deed of Trust** | Security instrument used in non-judicial foreclosure states |
| **Dormancy Period** | Time after which unclaimed property escheats to state |
| **Escheat** | Transfer of unclaimed property to state government |
| **Finder's Fee** | Fee for finding and referring a business opportunity |
| **Foreclosure** | Legal process of selling property to satisfy a debt |
| **Judicial Foreclosure** | Foreclosure supervised by a court |
| **Maintenance** | Improper interference in litigation by providing financial support |
| **Non-Judicial Foreclosure** | Foreclosure conducted without court supervision |
| **Pro Se** | Representing oneself without an attorney |
| **Right of Redemption** | Right to reclaim foreclosed property by paying debt |
| **Sheriff's Sale** | Foreclosure sale conducted by county sheriff |
| **Surplus** | Excess proceeds after paying all liens and costs |
| **Trustee's Sale** | Foreclosure sale conducted by a trustee (deed of trust) |
| **UPL** | Unauthorized Practice of Law |
| **Usury** | Charging interest above legal limit |
