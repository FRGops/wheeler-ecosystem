## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The single most important legal risk in surplus funds recovery is the **Unauthorized Practice of Law (UPL)** . Understanding which states require attorney involvement and what activities non-attorneys may perform is critical to designing a compliant operational model.

This document maps:
- Which states require attorney involvement for surplus claims
- Which states permit non-attorney participation
- Attorney licensing, pro hac vice, and MJP rules
- Fee splitting and referral fee rules by state
- A practical framework for building a multi-state attorney network

> **CRITICAL DISCLAIMER: This document is an operational playbook, NOT legal advice. Every conclusion herein requires verification by licensed counsel in the applicable jurisdiction. UPL determinations are notoriously fact-specific and subject to differing interpretations.**

## 4. STATES ALLOWING PRO SE CLAIMS

### 4.1 Pro Se by Claimant

In all states, the actual claimant (former homeowner) can theoretically appear pro se (represent themselves). However, in practice:

- **Tier 1 states:** Pro se filing by claimant is straightforward
- **Tier 2 states:** Pro se filing is permitted but procedures may be complex
- **Tier 3 states:** Pro se filing is permitted in theory but discouraged in practice

**Key Issue:** Pro se does NOT mean "non-attorney files for claimant." A non-attorney cannot prepare or file legal documents for another person in ANY state. The pro se exception only applies to the actual party.

### 4.2 Pro Se by Assignee

Some states allow the assignee (the company or person who has been assigned surplus rights) to file as a real party in interest:

| States | Rule |
|--------|------|
| **TX, GA, AL, MS, TN, SC, NC, VA, WV, KY, IN, OH, MI, MO, IA, MN, WI, IL, CO, AZ, NV, OR, WA, ID, UT, MT, WY, ND, SD, NE, KS, OK, NM** | Assignee may file as real party in interest under general rules of civil procedure |
| **CA, NY, FL, MA, NJ, LA** | Assignee filing may be restricted; court may require representation |

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** The ability of a corporate assignee to appear pro se is a corporation is not an individual and may not be able to appear pro se. This is a critical distinction. Some states require all corporations to be represented by counsel.

## 5. STATES ALLOWING NON-ATTORNEY FINDERS/HELPERS

### 5.1 Activities Generally Permissible for Non-Attorneys

In all states (including Tier 3), a non-attorney may generally:

1. **Identify surplus opportunities** through public records research
2. **Locate and contact potential claimants** (subject to marketing/solicitation restrictions)
3. **Refer claimants to licensed attorneys**
4. **Provide factual information** (e.g., "the court records show a surplus of $X,XXX from the sale on [date]")
5. **Provide administrative support** to licensed attorneys
6. **Conduct skip tracing** and background research
7. **Complete data entry** and organize documents

### 5.2 Activities That May Be UPL (Non-Attorney Cannot Do)

In ALL states, a non-attorney cannot:

1. **Prepare legal documents** for another person (petitions, motions, orders)
2. **Give legal advice** about rights, strategies, or legal consequences
3. **Appear in court** on behalf of another person
4. **Select and complete court forms** for another person
5. **Evaluate legal claims** or advise on claim value
6. **Negotiate legal rights** or settlement terms
7. **Hold oneself out as a lawyer** or legal service provider

## 6. ATTORNEY LICENSING REQUIREMENTS

### 6.1 General Rule

An attorney must be **licensed in the state** where the legal services are performed. For surplus funds recovery, this generally means:

- **Court filings:** Must be filed by an attorney licensed in the state where the court sits
- **Legal advice:** Must be given by an attorney licensed in the state where the advice is received
- **Negotiation:** Settlement or claim negotiation that involves legal rights generally requires in-state licensure

### 6.2 States With Specific In-State License Requirements

| State | Requirement | Exception |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **California** | CA Bar admission required for any appearance | Pro hac vice available for specific cases |
| **Florida** | FL Bar admission required | Pro hac vice with local counsel |
| **Louisiana** | LA Bar admission required | Pro hac vice available |
| **Massachusetts** | MA Bar admission | Pro hac vice available |
| **New Jersey** | NJ Bar admission | Pro hac vice available |
| **New York** | NY Bar admission | Pro hac vice with local counsel |
| **All others** | State-specific license for court appearance | Pro hac vice generally available |

### 6.3 Surplus-Specific Licensing Nuance

For the surplus recovery business, the key question is: **does the activity constitute the practice of law in [state]?**

- **Claim filing and court appearance:** This is the practice of law in EVERY state
- **Assignment document preparation:** This MAY be the practice of law in some states
- **Fee negotiation and agreement:** This MAY be the practice of law depending on the nature of the agreement
- **Claimant counseling:** Explaining legal rights and options is the practice of law in every state

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** The scope of "practice of law" varies by state. Have a licensed attorney in each state of operation confirm what activities require a law license.

### 7.2 Pro Hac Vice Limitations for Surplus Work

While pro hac vice is theoretically available in all states, practical limitations exist:

- **Frequency limits:** Some states limit how often an attorney can appear pro hac vice (e.g., no more than 5 cases per year in some states)
- **Local counsel requirement:** All states require local counsel — this means the attorney network must include local attorneys anyway
- **Case-by-case requirement:** Pro hac vice must be applied for each case, creating administrative overhead
- **Not suitable for high-volume:** If the business expects high claim volume in a state, a licensed in-state attorney is more practical than pro hac vice
- **Ethics compliance:** Pro hac vice attorneys must comply with the host state's ethics rules

> **Operational Recommendation:** Pro hac vice is not a practical solution for high-volume surplus recovery. For states where the business has meaningful volume, retain or affiliate with in-state licensed counsel.

## 8. MULTI-JURISDICTIONAL PRACTICE (MJP) RULES

MJP rules govern when an attorney may practice in a jurisdiction where they are not licensed.

### 8.1 Model Rule 5.5 — Key Provisions

ABA Model Rule 5.5 (adopted in some form by most states) provides:

- (a) A lawyer shall not practice law in a jurisdiction in violation of the regulation of the legal profession in that jurisdiction
- (b) A lawyer who is not admitted to practice in this jurisdiction shall not:
  - (1) establish an office or systematic and continuous presence; or
  - (2) hold out to the public as admitted to practice
- (c) A lawyer admitted in another US jurisdiction may provide legal services on a temporary basis that:
  - (1) are undertaken in association with a lawyer who is admitted to practice in this jurisdiction;
  - (2) are in or reasonably related to a pending proceeding before a tribunal;
  - (3) are in or reasonably related to a pending or potential arbitration, mediation, or other alternative dispute resolution proceeding; or
  - (4) are not within (2) or (3) and arise out of or are reasonably related to the lawyer's practice in a jurisdiction in which the lawyer is admitted

### 8.2 MJP Implications for Surplus Recovery

| Scenario | MJP Analysis |
|----------|--------------|
| In-state attorney files surplus claim | OK — fully licensed |
| Out-of-state attorney associates with in-state counsel for surplus claim | OK — temporary practice under Rule 5.5(c)(1) |
| Out-of-state attorney handles surplus claim without in-state counsel | NOT OK — unauthorized practice |
| Attorney gives legal advice to claimant via phone/email from another state | MAY BE OK if related to home state practice; RISKY if systematic |
| Attorney reviews documents for claimant in another state | RISKY — may constitute practice of law in claimant's state |

### 8.3 States Where MJP Is More Restrictive

| State | Restriction |
|-------|-------------|
| **California** | Strict MJP rules; out-of-state advice may be UPL |
| **Florida** | Florida Bar takes expansive view of what constitutes practice in Florida |
| **New York** | NY has strict MJP enforcement; in-person practice generally required |
| **New Jersey** | NJ limits temporary practice more than Model Rule |

> **ATTORNEY REVIEW REQUIRED:** MJP rules are complex and state-specific. The attorney network should include in-state counsel for each state where claims are filed.

## 9. ATTORNEY FEE SPLITTING RULES BY STATE

### 9.1 Model Rule 1.5(e) — Fee Splitting Between Lawyers

Lawyers may split fees with lawyers not in the same firm if:

1. The division is in proportion to the services performed by each lawyer, OR each lawyer assumes joint responsibility for the representation
2. The client agrees in writing to the arrangement (including the share each lawyer will receive)
3. The total fee is reasonable

### 9.2 State Adoption of Model Rule 1.5(e)

| Category | States |
|----------|--------|
| **Model Rule (proportional or joint responsibility)** | Most states |
| **Proportional required, no joint responsibility option** | California, District of Columbia |
| **More restrictive than Model Rule** | New York, New Jersey, Connecticut |
| **No specific rule** | Minor variations only |

### 9.3 Practical Implications for Surplus Recovery

When a surplus recovery company identifies a claimant and refers them to an attorney, if the attorney pays the company a fee:

**Scenario A: Referral fee (per-case payment from attorney to company)**
- **PROHIBITED in all states** if the company is not a lawyer — Model Rule 5.4(a) prohibits fee sharing with non-lawyers
- Exception: Some states allow referral fees for "lawyer referral services" that are certified by the state bar

**Scenario B: Marketing services fee (flat monthly or hourly fee)**
- **PERMISSIBLE** IF the fee is for bona fide marketing services at fair market value
- NOT permissible if the fee is actually a per-referral payment disguised as marketing
- Must be documented with a written agreement describing services and fee

**Scenario C: Attorney contracts directly with claimant, company provides admin support**
- **PERMISSIBLE** IF:
  - Attorney has direct engagement with claimant
  - Fee is the attorney's fee (governed by ethics rules)
  - Company charges attorney for administrative services at fair market value
  - No per-case kickback arrangement

**Scenario D: Company contracts with claimant, hires attorney as independent contractor**
- **HIGH RISK** — may be UPL (company practicing law through attorney)
- May also violate Rule 5.4 (fee splitting with non-lawyer)
- Generally NOT recommended

## 10. REFERRAL FEE RULES BY STATE

### 10.1 What States Allow Referral Fees Between Attorneys

| Rule | States |
|------|--------|
| **Pure referral fee allowed (no work required)** | CA, DC (limited) |
| **Referral fee requires proportional work or joint responsibility** | AL, AK, AZ, AR, CO, CT, DE, FL, GA, HI, ID, IL, IN, IA, KS, KY, LA, ME, MD, MA, MI, MN, MS, MO, MT, NE, NV, NH, NJ, NM, NY, NC, ND, OH, OK, OR, PA, RI, SC, SD, TN, TX, UT, VT, VA, WA, WV, WI, WY |
| **Referral fee prohibited outright** | None (all allow some form) |

### 10.2 What States Allow Referral Fees to Non-Attorneys

> **General Rule under Model Rule 5.4(a):** A lawyer or law firm shall not share legal fees with a non-lawyer.

| Category | States | Rule |
|----------|--------|------|
| **Prohibited** | All states that have adopted Rule 5.4 (49 states + DC) | No fee sharing with non-lawyers |
| **Permitted (limited exception)** | California (limited) | CA Rule 1.5.4 (formerly 1-320) prohibits general fee sharing but some referral arrangements may be structured through approved lawyer referral services |
| **Except: Certified Lawyer Referral Service** | Most states with certified LRIS | Payments to certified non-profit lawyer referral services permitted |

### 10.3 Surplus-Specific Referral Fee Analysis

For the surplus recovery business model:

**Direct Finder Model:**
- Company contracts with claimant for a percentage of surplus
- Company may or may not involve an attorney
- This is NOT an attorney referral fee — it's a direct contract with the claimant
- Legal analysis: Is this a valid assignment/finder fee? (covered in State Compliance Matrix)

**Attorney Referral Model:**
- Company identifies claimant, refers to attorney
- Attorney contracts with claimant for a fee
- Attorney pays company a referral fee
- **This is problematic** — paying non-attorneys for referrals typically violates ethics rules

**Marketing Services Model:**
- Company provides marketing to attorneys (e.g., leads generated through public records research)
- Attorney pays flat monthly fee or fair market value for services
- No per-case payment
- **This is the most compliant approach** for Tier 2/3 states

**Payment for "Going Business" or "Goodwill":**
- When an attorney purchases a surplus recovery business, they may pay for goodwill
- Must be a bona fide sale, not a disguised referral arrangement
- Must comply with Rule 1.17 (sale of law practice)

## 12. LEGAL ETHICS COMPLIANCE FRAMEWORK

### 12.1 Key Ethics Rules Affecting Surplus Recovery

| Rule | Subject | Impact |
|------|---------|--------|
| **Model Rule 1.5** | Fees | Reasonableness; disclosure; contingency fee rules |
| **Model Rule 1.8(a)** | Business transactions with client | Surplus assignment may be a business transaction with client — requires writing, fair terms, opportunity for independent counsel |
| **Model Rule 1.8(f)** | Third-party payment of fees | If company pays attorney fees for claimant, rules on third-party payments apply |
| **Model Rule 5.4** | Fee splitting with non-lawyer | Prohibits sharing legal fees with non-lawyers |
| **Model Rule 5.5** | UPL / MJP | Restricts practice where not licensed |
| **Model Rule 7.1-7.3** | Advertising and solicitation | Regulates attorney advertising and solicitation of clients |
| **Model Rule 8.4** | Misconduct | Prohibits dishonest or deceptive conduct |

### 12.2 The Ethical Surplus Recovery Company

To operate ethically and within legal boundaries:

**DO:**
- Clearly identify yourself and your business purpose when contacting claimants
- Provide written disclosures of all fees
- Recommend (but do not require) claimants to consult with independent counsel
- Obtain signed, written authorization before taking any action
- Respond promptly to claimant questions and concerns
- Maintain accurate records of all transactions
- Report and remit funds promptly
- Respect claimant's right to cancel or decline services

**DO NOT:**
- Misrepresent your role, qualifications, or the nature of the surplus
- Pressure or harass claimants into signing agreements
- Charge fees that are unconscionable or excessive
- Engage in conduct that could be construed as the practice of law
- File documents in court without proper authorization
- Commingle claimant funds with operating funds
- Make false or misleading statements about the claim process

### 12.3 Red Flags (Situations Requiring Immediate Attorney Referral)

| Situation | Risk | Action |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Claimant is deceased | Probate required | Refer to probate attorney; do NOT contract with heir without probate |
| Claimant is in bankruptcy | Automatic stay | Do NOT take any action; refer to bankruptcy attorney |
| Claimant is a minor | Guardian required | Do NOT contract with minor; refer to guardianship attorney |
| Claimant is incapacitated | Guardian/conservator | Do NOT contract directly |
| Claimant does not speak English | Language barrier | Provide translated documents; use certified interpreter |
| Competing claims exist | Heirs, creditors, judgment holders | Refer to litigation attorney |
| Foreclosure was defective | Litigation risk | Do NOT handle; refer to foreclosure defense attorney |
| Amount in question > $50,000 | Higher scrutiny | Enhanced documentation; attorney review |
| Claimant has active attorney | Ethics obligation | Do NOT contact them; communicate through their attorney |
